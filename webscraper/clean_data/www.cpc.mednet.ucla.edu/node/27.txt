<h3>
		Related Courses</h3>
<p>	<a href="/course/bls">CPR/Basic Life Support</a><br />
	<a href="/course/emt-preparation">EMT Preparation</a><br />
	<a href="/emt">Emergency Medical Technician</a><br />
	<a href="/course/paramedic-preparation">Paramedic Preparation</a><br />
	<a href="/paramedic">Paramedic Program</a></div>
<p>
	Whether it's on the breaking news or on a TV drama, you'll see them - EMS Personnel rescuing the victims of an accident or caring for a patient with a medical emergency.&nbsp;&nbsp;The majority of EMS responders are either certified at the EMT or Paramedic level.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Both work in a variety of roles and in several different healthcare settings.&nbsp;&nbsp;Ground ambulances, helicopters, industrial safety, and fire departments are often where they are employed.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;They both wear uniforms and they both help patients - so what's the difference?</p>
<p>
	In the field of emergency medical services (EMS), there are different levels of certifications for providers. Emergency Medical Technicians (EMTs) are the most common type of providers in EMS and are sometimes referred to as EMTs. EMTs learn the essential skills to help in life-threatening situations and many EMTs go on to earn an&nbsp;&nbsp;Advanced EMT certificate or become a Paramedic. Many doctors, nurses, and firefighters have also used their EMT education and work experience as stepping stones in their careers.&nbsp;</p>
<!--–– SECTION Infographic Block ––--><div class="row hubgrid equal" style="margin-bottom:20px; margin-top:20px; border-top: 1px solid #ccc; border-bottom: 1px solid #ccc; background-color: #eee; ">

<h2 style="margin-top:0;">
			Roadmap to Becoming an EMT or Paramedic</h2>
<p>
			Curious about launching your career in Emergency Medical Services with the nation's top EMT program? This infographic highlights the path to become an EMT and the path to continue on to be a paramedic.</p>
<p>
			<a class="btn btn-primary" href="/content/emt-paramedic-career-path" role="button" target="_blank">VIEW EMT/PARAMEDIC ROADMAP</a></p>
</div>
</div>
<p>
	The biggest difference between EMTs and Paramedics is the amount of education they receive and the level of care they provide&nbsp;&nbsp;for patients (i.e. scope of practice).&nbsp;</p>
<p>
	<strong>Emergency Medical Technician (EMT)&nbsp;</strong>students complete a course that is a minimum of 170 hours in length. EMTs are educated in assessing a patient and determining if any life threatening injuries or illnesses may be present.&nbsp;&nbsp;This includes splinting injuries for a patient following a motor vehicle collision, administering life saving epinephrine for a patient suffering an allergic reaction, or even administering CPR to a patient in cardiac arrest. Other skills the EMT will learn include oxygen administration, bag valve mask ventilations, delivery of a newborn, and even administration of several medications.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;An EMT’s assessment skills, the ability to quickly recognize if someone is dying, is the best tool in their tool box and the primary focus of the EMT education.&nbsp;</p>
<p>
	In general, to be eligible for enrollment into an EMT course you do&nbsp;<u>not</u>&nbsp;need to have any previous medical experience. The eligibility requirements and prerequisites for EMT and paramedic courses may vary from school to school. Be sure to check with the school’s and your state’s regulations before you enroll for a course. For example, in California you must be at least 18 years of age to be eligible to certify as an EMT.&nbsp;</p>
<p>
	<strong>Paramedic (PM)&nbsp;</strong>students complete a program between 1,200 to 1,800 hours and may last six to twelve months. Topics covered in paramedic courses include anatomy and physiology, cardiology, medications, and medical procedures. Paramedic courses build on EMT education and teach skills such as administering medications, starting intravenous lines, providing advanced airway management, EKG Interpretation for patients, and learning to provide emergency care to patients with&nbsp;&nbsp;life-threatening medical or traumatic emergencies.&nbsp;&nbsp;Caring for the victims of a motor vehicle crash, interpreting the EKG of a heart attack patient, or delivering a baby; these are all patients a paramedic must be prepared to assist during their shift.&nbsp;&nbsp;It never gets boring!&nbsp;&nbsp;Through a combination of lectures, skills labs, followed by hospital internship, then EMS field internship, students are prepared to pass the national certification exams to achieve the highest certification level of pre-hospital care provider in the United States.</p>
<p>
	To be eligible for a paramedic course you must be an EMT and generally have at least 6 months of work experience as an EMT. Paramedic Programs may have different admission requirements.&nbsp;&nbsp;Some require you to take college level Anatomy and Physiology prior to admission, many have personal health requirements such as proof of immunizations and a physical as an admission requirement.&nbsp;&nbsp;Schools may also require a criminal background check, admissions interview, or an entrance exam to determine your eligibility for admission.&nbsp;&nbsp;Each school has slightly different requirements but the goal is to select those candidates who will be successful in the training program.&nbsp;&nbsp;.&nbsp;</p>
<p>
	<strong>How Do I Become an EMT or a Paramedic?</strong></p>
<p>
	In order to become an EMT or a Paramedic, there are five (5) major steps that you will need to complete, which may vary from state to state. In California you must:</p>
<ol>
<li>
		Meet the eligibility requirements and prerequisites for attending an&nbsp;<a href="https://www.cpc.mednet.ucla.edu/emt/prerequisites">EMT</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a href="https://www.cpc.mednet.ucla.edu/course/paramedic/eligibility">Paramedic&nbsp;</a>course, including&nbsp;<a href="https://www.cpc.mednet.ucla.edu/course/bls">Basic Life Support</a>&nbsp;(BLS).&nbsp;</li>
<li>
		Attend and successfully complete an approved&nbsp;<a href="https://www.cpc.mednet.ucla.edu/cpc/course/emt">EMT</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a href="https://www.cpc.mednet.ucla.edu/paramedic">Paramedic</a>&nbsp;course.</li>
<li>
		Take and pass the&nbsp;<a href="https://www.nremt.org/rwd/public">National Registry of Emergency Medical Technicians</a>&nbsp;(NREMT) EMT or Paramedic computer based exam.</li>
<li>
		Take and pass the National Registry of Emergency Medical Technicians (NREMT) EMT or Paramedic skills exams.</li>
<li>
		Apply and obtain for certification within two years of being issued a course completion certificate.</li>
</ol>
