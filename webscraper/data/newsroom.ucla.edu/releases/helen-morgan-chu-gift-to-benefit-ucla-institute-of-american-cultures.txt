<p class="BodyA"><span><span><span><span><b><span><span><span></span></span></span></b></span></span></span></span><span style="font-family:Georgia,serif;"><b>Key takeaways</b></span></p>

<p class="BodyA"><span style="font-family:Georgia,serif;"></span></p>
</div>

<ul>
	<li><span style="font-family:Georgia,serif;">Longtime donors to UCLA, the Chus participated in student-led protests in the 1960s that brought about the creation of UCLA&rsquo;s ethnic studies centers.</span></li>
	<li><span style="font-family:Georgia,serif;">Their latest gift is intended to create four endowed chairs and provide wider support for the work of the UCLA Institute of American Cultures.</span></li>
	<li><span style="font-family:Georgia,serif;">The funding will help the campus remain a leader at a critical time for research and programs related to race and ethnic studies and Native and Indigenous studies.</span></li>
</ul>

<hr />
<p><span><span></span></span><span><span>Helen and Morgan Chu, whose student activism in the late 1960s helped launch UCLA&rsquo;s ethnic studies centers, have pledged $10 million to the UCLA Institute of American Cultures, the largest gift ever made to the institute. </span></span></p>

<p><span><span>The Chus have been longtime supporters of the university that brought them together more than five decades ago. Their previous philanthropy led to the creation of the Morgan and Helen Chu Chair in Asian American Studies, the Helen and Morgan Chu Director&rsquo;s Chair of the Asian American Studies Center and the Morgan &amp; Helen Chu Scholarship Fund for undergraduate students.</span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span>The <a href="https://iac.ucla.edu/">Institute of American Cultures</a> was founded in 1969 to serve as the central hub of the university&rsquo;s four ethnic studies centers, which are dedicated to research, community-based partnerships and civic engagement that advances social and racial justice. Through these efforts &mdash; and through events, conferences, grants, fellowships and scholarships &mdash; the centers, their students and affiliated faculty members are helping to shape social and cultural realities in the U.S. and beyond. </span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span>&ldquo;We are honored and humbled by Helen and Morgan Chu<span dir="RTL" lang="AR-SA">&rsquo;</span>s remarkable gift, which will advance UCLA<span dir="RTL" lang="AR-SA">&rsquo;</span>s scholarship and teaching related to human identities and some of the most pressing issues of our time,&rdquo; said UCLA Chancellor Gene Block. &ldquo;UCLA has long been at the forefront of the examination of the histories, cultures, contributions and experiences of different racial and ethnic groups in the United States, and the Chus&rsquo; investment will allow us to deepen the impact of this essential work.&rdquo;</span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span>The Chus&rsquo; gift is intended to endow an academic chair at the <a href="https://www.aasc.ucla.edu/">Asian American Studies Center</a> and three directors&rsquo; chairs &mdash; at the <a href="https://main.aisc.ucla.edu/">American Indian Studies Center,</a> the <a href="https://www.chicano.ucla.edu/">Chicano Studies Research Center</a> and the <a href="https://bunchecenter.ucla.edu/">Ralph J. Bunche Center for African American Studies.</a>&nbsp;A portion of the gift will also fund research projects and programming across the institute that will benefit faculty, students and other stakeholders. </span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span>The investments UCLA will make thanks to the Chus&rsquo; gift are aligned with the university<span dir="RTL" lang="AR-SA">&rsquo;</span>s commitment to <a href="https://equity.ucla.edu/edi-ucla/">inclusive excellence</a> and engaged scholarship, including its <a href="https://chancellor.ucla.edu/messages/rising-to-the-challenge/">Rising to the Challenge</a> and <a href="https://chancellor.ucla.edu/messages/announcing-the-native-american-and-pacific-islander-bruins-rising-initiative/">Native American and Pacific Islander Bruins Rising</a> initiatives. In addition, UCLA is currently pursuing official federal designations as a <a href="https://hsi.ucla.edu/">Hispanic-Serving Institution</a> and an <a href="https://www2.ed.gov/programs/aanapi/index.html">Asian American and Native American Pacific Islander&ndash;Serving Institution.</a></span></span><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span class="h3">The Chus: Bridging the gulfs that separate people</span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span>During their time as undergraduates, the couple were part of the multiracial coalition of student leaders whose dedicated activism and advocacy ultimately led to the founding of UCLA<span dir="RTL" lang="AR-SA">&rsquo;</span>s ethnic studies centers in 1969 &mdash; a moment that established UCLA as a pioneer in the field. </span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span><span><span><span><span><span><span><span>Helen Chu earned her bachelor&rsquo;s degree from UCLA and went on to a long career as a public school teacher.&nbsp;Morgan Chu earned bachelor&rsquo;s, master&rsquo;s and doctoral degrees from UCLA before earning a master&rsquo;s degree from Yale University and a juris doctorate from Harvard Law School.&nbsp;A specialist in intellectual property, he is one of the nation&rsquo;s preeminent trial lawyers.</span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<div class="textImage textImage-left textImage-original"><img align="left" alt="Head-and-shoulders photo of Morgan Chu (left) and Helen Chu standing outside, with trees and plants in background" author="Karen Umemoto/UCLA Asian American Studies Center" data-imgheight="878" data-imgwidth="1170" file_id="6552b8582cfac21f9a7b805a" src="https://s3.amazonaws.com/cms.ipressroom.com/173/files/202310/Chu-couple+photo.jpg" title="With their continued support of the UCLA Institute of American Cultures, Helen (right) and Morgan Chu aim to ensure that UCLA remains at the vanguard of ethnic studies." />
<div class="textImage-creditBody">Karen Umemoto/UCLA Asian American Studies Center</div>

<div class="textImage-captionBody">With their continued support of the UCLA Institute of American Cultures, Helen (right) and Morgan Chu aim to ensure that UCLA remains at the vanguard of ethnic studies.</div>
</div>

<div>&nbsp;</div>

<p><span><span></span></span><span><span>Through the years, the Chus have remained committed to the ethnic studies centers&rsquo; foundational mission of uplifting and fostering understanding of communities of color through scholarship and social impact work, which today has become increasingly collaborative and interdisciplinary &mdash; through intercenter efforts like an ethnic studies curriculum initiative and training program for K&ndash;12 educators &mdash; as well as international and transnational in its scope. </span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span>&ldquo;Over the course of 50 years, ethnic studies at UCLA has grown from a handful of faculty positions to numbering hundreds of affiliated faculty across all fields,&rdquo; said David Yoo, vice provost of the institute and a professor of Asian American studies and history at UCLA. &ldquo;This important gift from Helen and Morgan Chu will provide our faculty, students and community partners with sustainable support as they produce the knowledge we need in order to envision and build a more just and equitable society and world.&rdquo;</span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span>&ldquo;Morgan and I believe that the gulfs that persist in separating people can be bridged through study and research but also through conversation and, ultimately, mutual understanding,&rdquo; Helen Chu said. &ldquo;We are proud to make a gift that will help the faculty, students and staff associated with the UCLA Institute of American Cultures to advance social justice causes in the decades to come.&rdquo;</span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span>Helen Chu is a lifetime member of Women and Philanthropy at UCLA. Morgan Chu has served on The UCLA Foundation Board of Governors and has been a lecturer and commencement speaker at UCLA School of Law. He received the UCLA Medal, the university&rsquo;s highest honor, in 2007. </span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span>&ldquo;At this important juncture for ethnic studies in American higher education, our purpose with this gift is nothing less than bolstering rigorous scholarship in these fields at UCLA,&rdquo; Morgan Chu said. &ldquo;We chose to make our gift here because of UCLA<span dir="RTL" lang="AR-SA">&rsquo;</span>s ability to have a meaningful impact and because the institution has meant so much to us throughout our academic and personal lives.&rdquo;</span></span></p>
</div>

<p><span class="h3">Research that shapes realities</span></p>

<p><span><span>UCLA&rsquo;s ethnic studies centers conduct a wide array of research, advocacy, education and community engagement projects. Here is a sampling of some of their recent efforts.</span></span></p>

<p><strong><span><span></span></span></strong><span><span><strong>American Indian Studies Center:</strong> A partnership with the Fowler Museum at UCLA, <a href="https://www.coah-repat.com/home">Carrying Our Ancestors Home</a> focuses on the repatriation of Native ancestral remains and cultural items held in museums and other institutions, with guidance from Native cultural practitioners, tribal members, museum professionals and academics.</span></span></p>

<p><strong><span><span></span></span></strong><span><span><strong>Asian American Studies Center: </strong>The center is currently developing &ldquo;Foundations and Futures,&rdquo; a <a href="https://newsroom.ucla.edu/releases/asian-american-studies-center-free-resource-high-school-teachers">multimedia, open-access textbook</a> featuring 50 chapters and 250 lesson plans to&nbsp;bring the experiences, histories, struggles, cultures and contributions of Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders into the curriculum of every high school and college classroom across the U.S.</span></span><span><span></span></span><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span><strong>Ralph J. Bunche Center for African American Studies: </strong>This fall, the center welcomed its fifth and largest <a href="https://bunchecenter.ucla.edu/research/bunche-fellows-program/">Bunche Fellows Program</a> cohort, connecting a community of 40 undergraduate and graduate students with 20 faculty-led research projects on a range of scholarly topics related to Black life in the U.S. and internationally.</span></span></p>

<p><span><span></span></span></p>

<p><span><span><strong>Chicano Studies Research Center: </strong>In collaboration with UCLA&rsquo;s Latino Policy and Politics Institute, the center recently launched the <a href="https://www.chicano.ucla.edu/research/latina-futures-2050-lab">Latina Futures, 2050 Lab,</a> which aims to accelerate equity and inclusion for Latinas through rigorous research, community partnerships and leadership development.&nbsp;</span></span></p>
